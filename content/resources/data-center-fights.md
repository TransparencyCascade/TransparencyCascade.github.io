If you've watched a hyperscale data center go up near you — or watched your electric bill climb and wondered why — you are not imagining it, and you are not alone. **AI data centers are now being fought, and increasingly stopped, by communities across the country.** This page is for the person who finished one of our pieces, or an episode, and asked: *where is this being resisted, and how do I find the fight near me?*

We are not going to build a tracker that already exists. Several well-resourced efforts are already mapping these fights better than we could from scratch — so the most useful thing we can do is point you at the good ones, tell you what's real, and add the one connection the rest of the coverage misses.

---

## The resistance is real, and it's working

The opposition has gone from scattered to structural. As of early 2026, [Data Center Watch](https://www.datacenterwatch.org/) counted **833 active opposition groups across 49 states**, and the fights are landing: opponents blocked or delayed at least **75 projects worth roughly $130 billion in a single quarter (Q1 2026)** — as much in three months as in all of 2025. By that spring, moratorium proposals had been introduced in 14 states, and **at least 69 local governments had enacted construction bans or pauses**, Seattle the biggest city among them. Virginia's "Data Center Alley" remains the national concentration point — where most voters now blame the facilities for rising electricity costs — while Maryland, Ohio, and Texas had the most active opposition groups.

The point worth holding onto: **"you can't fight this" is simply false.** People are fighting it, and winning more than the news admits.

## Where to find the fights (the trackers)

These are the efforts already doing the count-and-map work. Use them to find the campaign near you, see what's been won, and learn the tactics.

- **[Data Center Watch](https://www.datacenterwatch.org/)** — the leading tracker, run by the intelligence firm 10a Labs. Quantified, current, with regular briefings on blocked and delayed projects and the dollar figures behind them. Start here for the national picture.
- **Data Center Opposition** — a newer coalition platform organizing the community-group side: a directory of hundreds of local opposition groups across dozens of states. The place to find or list a *group*, not just a project.
- **[Heatmap Pro / Heatmap News](https://heatmap.news/)** — a researched fights database plus the strongest reporting on the politics of the buildout and the local-opposition beat.

## What the fights are actually about (the objection vectors)

The campaigns that work tend to fight on concrete, local ground rather than abstract opposition to AI:

- **Water.** Data centers consume enormous quantities of water for cooling. One analysis projected Texas data centers drawing tens of billions of gallons a year, rising toward hundreds of billions by 2030. In drought-stressed regions this is often the strongest argument.
- **Electricity costs and the grid.** A Bloomberg analysis found electricity costs near data centers running dramatically higher than five years earlier; national data-center demand is projected to nearly double by 2028. Ratepayers — not the tech companies — frequently absorb the cost, which is why "your bill is subsidizing their server farm" lands politically.
- **Tax abatements vs. jobs.** Data centers are often granted large tax breaks while creating very few permanent local jobs — an extractive deal that local officials can be pressed on.
- **Zoning, noise, and farmland.** The land-use fight, where local ordinances and review processes give communities real leverage.

For the authoritative versions of these arguments, the consumer- and ratepayer-impact research from **[Consumer Reports](https://www.consumerreports.org/data-centers/)** and the land-and-water analysis from the **[Lincoln Institute of Land Policy](https://www.lincolninst.edu/)** are good places to start.

## The connection most coverage misses

Here is the part you will not find on a general data-center tracker, because it requires connecting two stories usually kept separate.

**The data center and the ICE detention warehouse are, in places, the same building — built by the same developers, sometimes on the same parcel.** In our reporting we have documented companies selling the *same kind of speculative warehouse shell* to ICE as a detention facility or to an AI firm as a data center, depending on which buyer shows up — and in at least one case, retrofitting the very same building. The body-prison and the attention-prison run on the same concrete, the same capital, and the same playbook of local incentives and bypassed review.

That means the data-center fight and the detention fight are not separate causes. They are the same fight against the same extraction, and the communities running each have more to learn from each other than they usually know.

- Our reporting on the convergence: [The Power the Pope Warned About Has a Guest List](https://theramm.transparencycascade.org/p/the-power-the-pope-warned-about-has) and [The Sharkov System](https://theramm.transparencycascade.org/p/the-sharkov-system-the-99-million).
- The detention side of the same machine, tracked fight by fight: our [Detention Pipeline](https://detention-pipeline.transparencycascade.org/fights/) — 176 local campaigns against ICE detention facilities, dozens already won.
